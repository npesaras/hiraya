CHAPTER 2
REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE AND RELATED SYSTEMS

This chapter reviews the literature and existing systems relevant to the development of the Faculty Scholarship Grant Management and Evaluation System (FSMES). Consistent with the applied nature of the study, the chapter does not revolve around abstract theory testing. Instead, it examines the bodies of knowledge that justify the need for the system, inform its major design choices, and clarify the limitations of currently available tools and platforms. The discussion begins with digital transformation in higher education administration, narrows to centralized workflow management, role-based access control, user-centered system development, and usability evaluation, and then examines related systems relevant to scholarship, grant, or faculty workflow management. The chapter ends with a synthesis that identifies the specific applied gap addressed by FSMES.

2.1 Review of Related Literature
2.1.1 Digital Transformation in Higher Education Administration

Digital transformation in higher education is no longer limited to online classes, learning management systems, or digital library resources. It also includes the redesign of administrative processes so that institutions can provide more efficient, responsive, and accountable services to students, faculty, researchers, and administrative units. The World Bank's report on Philippine higher education emphasizes that digital transformation in the sector must involve common and shared platforms and services for academic, research, and administrative stakeholders rather than isolated tools adopted only for immediate convenience (World Bank, 2022). In the context of this capstone, this is important because faculty scholarship administration is not a single-office task. It is a multi-actor institutional workflow that depends on structured records, sequence, and role-sensitive actions.

The broader literature supports this same position. Gkrimpizi et al. (2023) identify numerous barriers that hinder digital transformation in higher education institutions, including organizational, technological, people-related, and cultural barriers. Their findings matter for the present study because they show that institutions do not fail at digitalization simply because they lack software. They also fail when the systems they use do not match their actual administrative structures and processes. For FSMES, digitalization is only meaningful if the platform reflects the real procedural logic of the IASP scholarship workflow at MSU-IIT.

2.1.2 Centralized Record Management, Status Visibility, and the Limits of Fragmented Workflows

Administrative workflows become inefficient when documents, decisions, and status updates are distributed across paper files, email threads, and separate spreadsheets rather than maintained in a unified environment. In higher education, this problem becomes more serious when a process involves multiple stages, formal approvals, and documentary requirements that must be satisfied in order. The World Bank (2022) underscores that stronger administrative and management capacities are part of higher education digital transformation, which implies that institutions must move beyond disconnected practices and toward systems that can support coordinated processes.

For a faculty scholarship workflow like the IASP process, fragmented records create practical risks because confirming case progression, document completeness, and the currently valid document set becomes harder when records are dispersed across separate channels. This matters directly to FSMES because the system is not just a data repository. It must support orderly movement through required stages of submission, review, and decision while maintaining full and accurate workflow records that can be checked and traced when needed (Gkrimpizi et al., 2023; Republic of the Philippines, 2007). Therefore, a centralized workflow platform is justified not merely for convenience, but for process reliability, status visibility, and administrative traceability.

2.1.3 Role-Based Access Control and Workflow Enforcement

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) is one of the most relevant concepts for institutional workflow systems because it links system permissions to organizational roles rather than granting unrestricted access to all users. Sandhu et al. (1996) explain RBAC as a model in which permissions are assigned to roles and users are then associated with those roles according to their job responsibilities. This is highly relevant to FSMES because the scholarship workflow involves different users with different responsibilities. Faculty applicants, panel members, and administrators should not all have access to the same actions, records, or process controls.

RBAC alone, however, is not enough if the workflow itself is not structured. In stage-based institutional processes, the system must also preserve sequence, accountability, and traceability. Uddin et al. (2019) show that workflow-aware access control strengthens policy enforcement by linking permissions to task progression and real-time governance requirements. This is consistent with the National Archives of the Philippines Act of 2007, which requires full and accurate records, records management audits, and protected access controls across government offices, including state universities and colleges (Republic of the Philippines, 2007). In applied terms, this matters to FSMES because the system needs both role-bounded action and traceable progression within the submission and panel evaluation stages. The platform must therefore do more than accept files. It must preserve workflow logic, limit actions by role, and maintain a record of workflow activity.

2.1.4 User-Centered Development Through Design Sprint

The Design Sprint is an appropriate approach for applied system development because it begins with a practical problem, narrows that problem using stakeholder evidence, and moves toward a testable prototype through a structured sequence. Knapp et al. (2016) describe the sprint as a rapid but disciplined method for solving problems and testing ideas in a short development cycle. The value of this approach in a capstone project lies in its ability to prevent feature invention based on developer assumption.

For FSMES, this is important because the system is not intended to be a generic scholarship portal. It is a purpose-built response to the actual workflow difficulties experienced in the IASP process at MSU-IIT. Maguire (2001) argues that human-centred methods produce stronger system requirements because design decisions are grounded in direct user input rather than in developer assumption. Through the logic of the Design Sprint, the researchers can move from workflow pain points to a focused problem definition, then to interface ideas, a functional prototype, and stakeholder-based testing. For this capstone, that keeps the development process tied to the documented local workflow problem that FSMES is intended to address.

2.1.5 Usability Evaluation in Institutional System Development

Usability evaluation is a necessary part of applied system development because a prototype must be tested with representative users performing realistic tasks, not judged only by its technical completion. Nielsen (1994) argued that usability problems can often be identified through tests with small numbers of representative users, making structured prototype evaluation practical even in small-scale development projects. This matters to FSMES because the system is intended for actual faculty applicants and panel members whose task performance can show whether the workflow is understandable and workable in practice.

Post-study usability measurement also requires a validated instrument that captures how users perceive the system after task completion. Lewis (1995) introduced the Post-Study System Usability Questionnaire (PSSUQ) and reported strong psychometric properties for the instrument, while Lewis (2002) further evaluated the questionnaire using five years of usability-study data and confirmed its usefulness across the dimensions of system usefulness, information quality, and interface quality. For FSMES, this makes the PSSUQ appropriate because the study needs structured evidence not only that the prototype functions, but also that users find it understandable, usable, and appropriate for the institutional workflow it supports.

2.2 Related Systems

Existing systems related to scholarship, grants, and faculty administration show that digital platforms can support application intake, review coordination, and record management. However, similarity in function does not automatically mean suitability for the specific workflow of the Academic Scholarship Panel at MSU-IIT. The following systems are selected because they support scholarship or faculty-related application intake, involve centralized records or multi-stage review handling, and/or operate in higher education or comparable institutional settings.

2.2.1 CHED Student Financial Assistance Programs (STUFAPs) Portal

The Commission on Higher Education maintains a Student Financial Assistance Programs portal that organizes scholarship and grant opportunities such as merit-based and financial assistance programs in one official digital environment (Commission on Higher Education [CHED], n.d.). The relevance of this platform to FSMES lies in its demonstration that scholarship-related services benefit from centralization, visibility, and structured access to program requirements.

Its limitation in the context of this capstone is scope and user type. The CHED portal is student-oriented and national in scope. It is not designed for faculty-specific scholarship governance within a university, nor does it appear to implement the internal role-bounded approval workflow required by a local institutional process such as the APDP. Therefore, while it supports the value of centralized scholarship management, it does not directly answer the workflow problem addressed by FSMES. For FSMES, the design implication is to retain centralized intake and requirement visibility while narrowing the workflow to faculty users and policy-bound internal review.

2.2.2 Academic Works

Academic Works, as implemented through university scholarship portals such as the Stephen F. Austin State University scholarships page, provides a centralized online environment where users complete a general application and respond to additional scholarship-specific requirements when needed (Stephen F. Austin State University, n.d.). This makes it relevant to FSMES because it demonstrates how a shared platform can streamline scholarship application intake and reduce dependence on fragmented submission practices.

Its limitation for the present study is contextual fit. Academic Works is a broad scholarship management platform primarily oriented toward scholarship opportunities in a university setting, but the publicly accessible implementation does not show a workflow specifically designed for faculty scholarship evaluation under a local policy-bound approval chain such as the APDP. In other words, it is a useful example of centralized scholarship application handling, but not a direct model for the institutional workflow enforcement required by FSMES. For FSMES, the design implication is to keep streamlined application intake while adding faculty-specific role logic and local evaluation flow.

2.2.3 OpenWater

OpenWater presents itself as an end-to-end scholarship management system and explicitly contrasts its integrated workflow with traditional arrangements in which organizations still handle tracking, judging, and related logistics manually through email or spreadsheets (OpenWater, n.d.). This is highly relevant to FSMES because it directly illustrates the weakness of disconnected administrative practices in scholarship or application review environments. It also supports the practical value of combining submission, review, and coordination within one system.

Its limitation in the context of this study is specificity. OpenWater is a configurable general-purpose platform designed to support many kinds of application and review programs, including scholarships, grants, awards, and fellowships. FSMES, by contrast, is designed for a narrowly defined faculty scholarship process governed by a specific institutional policy and local approval structure. As a result, OpenWater is useful as a benchmark for integrated workflow capability, but it does not by itself solve the policy-aligned, role-bounded workflow problem faced by the IASP at MSU-IIT. For FSMES, the design implication is to integrate submission and review within one platform while constraining the workflow to the actual IASP roles and rules.

2.2.4 Palawan State University Faculty Information Management System

Palawan State University maintains a Faculty Information Management System that is publicly labeled as a Faculty Online Records Management System (Palawan State University, n.d.). This system is relevant because it demonstrates that faculty-related records can be managed through a dedicated institutional platform rather than through general-purpose or student-focused tools.

Its limitation for FSMES is functional scope. Based on the publicly visible description, the PSU system is oriented toward faculty records management rather than toward scholarship application intake, panel evaluation, and workflow-sensitive decision handling. Therefore, it is useful as evidence that faculty administrative processes merit dedicated systems, but it does not directly address the specific faculty scholarship workflow problem that FSMES is designed to solve. For FSMES, the design implication is to retain dedicated faculty-oriented administration while extending the platform beyond records storage into scholarship workflow handling.

The reviewed systems show that digital scholarship management and faculty administration are already well-established directions in institutional practice. However, none of the reviewed systems is specifically designed for the submission, review, and status-tracking requirements of the IASP process at MSU-IIT. That limitation is what makes FSMES necessary as a context-specific system rather than a generic scholarship portal or a general records platform.

2.3 Literature Synthesis

The literature reviewed in this chapter establishes several points that justify the development of FSMES. First, digital transformation in higher education must include administrative services and not only academic delivery systems (World Bank, 2022). Second, the success of digital transformation depends not just on adopting technology, but on aligning systems with actual institutional workflows and overcoming fragmentation, organizational barriers, and process mismatch (Gkrimpizi et al., 2023). Third, institutional workflow systems require more than document storage. They require role-based access structures, workflow-aware control, and traceable record activity so that actions occur in the correct sequence and within the bounds of user responsibility (Sandhu et al., 1996; Uddin et al., 2019; Republic of the Philippines, 2007). Fourth, user-centered development approaches such as the Design Sprint are appropriate for applied capstones because they move from real user problems to practical system design decisions through a structured process grounded in stakeholder input (Knapp et al., 2016; Maguire, 2001). Fifth, usability evaluation through representative-user testing and validated post-study instruments such as the PSSUQ provides structured evidence of whether a prototype is understandable, usable, and appropriate for its intended institutional users (Nielsen, 1994; Lewis, 1995; Lewis, 2002).

The related systems further show that while digital scholarship and faculty platforms already exist, they do not directly resolve the exact problem addressed in this study. Student scholarship portals centralize program access but do not reflect faculty-specific internal approval chains. General scholarship management platforms support application review but remain configurable rather than policy-bound to the APDP context. Faculty information systems demonstrate the value of dedicated faculty administration but do not necessarily support scholarship evaluation workflows. Despite the documented benefits of digital scholarship management, no reviewed system is designed specifically for the submission, review, and status-tracking requirements of the IASP process at MSU-IIT. This constitutes the specific applied gap that the development of FSMES addresses.
