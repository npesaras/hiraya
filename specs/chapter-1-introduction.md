CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION

Higher education institutions are under increasing pressure to modernize administrative processes that remain dependent on paper records, fragmented communication, and manual approvals. In the Philippine higher education sector, digital transformation is no longer confined to teaching and learning platforms; it also includes the redesign of administrative services so that institutions can deliver more efficient, accountable, and user-responsive operations (World Bank, 2022). This shift is especially important in workflows that involve multiple offices, recurring documentary requirements, and decision points that must be completed in sequence.

One of the administrative areas that clearly requires this form of modernization is scholarship and grant management. These workflows involve document submission, eligibility verification, multistage review, status monitoring, and formal recording of decisions. When these activities are handled through paper folders, email exchanges, and disconnected spreadsheets, the result is not merely inconvenience but a structurally weak process in which records are dispersed, process visibility is low, and accountability becomes difficult to establish. Studies on digital transformation in higher education show that fragmented systems, organizational complexity, and weak process alignment continue to hinder effective administrative digitalization in universities (Gkrimpizi et al., 2023; World Bank, 2022).

The people most directly affected by these workflow weaknesses are the stakeholders who participate in each stage of the process. In faculty scholarship administration, these include faculty applicants, department and college endorsers, panel members, and administrative offices responsible for organizing, evaluating, and monitoring cases. In a workflow of this kind, each actor is allowed to perform only particular actions at specific stages. For this reason, the system supporting the process must do more than store information. It must also enforce access boundaries and role-specific actions in a way that reflects the actual institutional structure. This is the practical relevance of role-based access control, which connects user permissions to organizational responsibilities rather than leaving process discipline to informal coordination alone (Sandhu et al., 1996).

At Mindanao State University-Iligan Institute of Technology (MSU-IIT), this issue is evident in the administration of faculty scholarship grants under the Academic Personnel Development Program (APDP). For the application submission and panel evaluation stages covered in this study, the APDP is operationalized through the Institute Academic Scholarship Panel (IASP), which is tasked with interviewing, screening, and supervising applicants under institutional policy. The process involves documentary compliance, departmental and college endorsement, panel evaluation, and decision routing across a structured administrative chain (MSU-IIT, 2021). Yet despite this formal structure, day-to-day implementation still relies on manual submission, physical document handling, office-based coordination, and informal follow-up practices.

This creates a clear applied problem. General-purpose tools such as email, shared folders, and spreadsheets may support isolated administrative tasks, but they do not enforce the sequential, role-bounded workflow required in the IASP application submission and evaluation stages. They do not provide a centralized and authoritative record of each application, do not automatically structure actions by role, and do not offer reliable status visibility throughout the scoped workflow. As a result, the scholarship process remains vulnerable to delays, record fragmentation, repetitive follow-up, and weak process traceability. These conditions point to the need for a purpose-built digital platform designed specifically for the scholarship submission and panel evaluation workflow within the IASP process at MSU-IIT. The Faculty Scholarship Grant Management and Evaluation System (FSMES) is developed using the Design Sprint methodology, which ensures that every system feature is grounded in the documented pain points of its actual users rather than in developer assumptions (Knapp et al., 2016). The following section presents the operational context, current process, and specific institutional problems that justify the proposed development of FSMES.

1.1 Background of the Study

At Mindanao State University-Iligan Institute of Technology (MSU-IIT), the academic advancement of the teaching faculty is formally supported through the Academic Personnel Development Program (APDP), an institutional mechanism that governs fellowship and scholarship grants for graduate studies and specialized training. The program operates under Board of Regents Resolution No. 3, series of 1992, as revised in September 2021, with the Revised Guidelines on Academic Personnel Development Program serving as the governing institutional reference for the process (MSU-IIT, 2021). The program is therefore not simply a scholarship opportunity. It is a policy-bound faculty development mechanism whose implementation affects instructional capability, departmental planning, and institutional growth.

The APDP is operationalized through the Institute Academic Scholarship Panel (IASP), a formally constituted body responsible for screening, interviewing, evaluating, and supervising faculty scholarship applicants. The panel is chaired by the Vice Chancellor for Academic Affairs and works with other institutional representatives involved in legal, budgetary, human resource, and faculty concerns. Because APDP grants involve tuition support, stipends, allowances, and return-service obligations, the quality, traceability, and consistency of the evaluation process are institutionally significant.

Before a faculty member can be considered for a grant, several documentary and procedural requirements must be satisfied. Based on the official guidelines, applicants must be permanent employees with at least a Very Satisfactory teaching efficiency rating for each semester of the last two academic years, and they must submit documents such as a formal letter request, department and dean endorsement, teaching efficiency records, notice of admission or program of study, transcripts, and employment records. The Department Chairperson and College Dean must also certify that the proposed study aligns with the unit's faculty development plan and that the official leave will not compromise departmental teaching requirements. This means that a scholarship application is not a one-step submission but a multilevel institutional process involving documentary preparation, endorsement, review, and decision recording.

In practice, the current scholarship application process remains largely manual and paper-based. Faculty applicants prepare physical documentary requirements, secure signatures from the required offices, and personally submit the complete set within the prescribed timeline. After submission, records are handled through physical folders, office-based coordination, and scattered digital support rather than through a single dedicated platform. Applicants must submit the documentary set not later than six months before the intended start of study leave, which adds time sensitivity to an already document-heavy workflow.

This implementation creates practical breakdowns at both the applicant and administrative levels. For applicants, the process creates uncertainty because there is no reliable system-based means of knowing whether an application has already been received, found complete, scheduled for review, or acted upon by the panel. For administrative personnel and panel members, the absence of a centralized environment makes it difficult to track case progress, confirm documentary completeness, and maintain consolidated records of panel action. These weaknesses are consistent with broader higher education digitalization barriers identified in the literature, particularly where fragmented systems and weak process alignment persist (Gkrimpizi et al., 2023; World Bank, 2022).

In response to these limitations, offices often rely on email coordination, spreadsheets, and shared document folders to reduce the physical burden of handling files. These ad hoc tools may help users share scanned documents and track basic lists across offices, but they do not constitute a workflow system. They do not enforce the required sequence of approval, do not define user permissions according to formal institutional roles, and do not generate a single authoritative record of actions taken at each stage of the process.

The problem at MSU-IIT, therefore, is not simply that the scholarship process uses paper. The deeper problem is that the process lacks a purpose-built digital platform capable of translating the APDP's procedural requirements into an enforced, traceable, and centralized workflow. The scholarship administration problem is structural rather than a matter of individual effort, and no existing general-purpose tool currently used in the process can fully resolve it. This is the context that justifies the proposed development of FSMES.

1.2 Statement of the Problem

Faculty development programs are operationally important because they support the upgrading of qualifications, strengthen department capability, and contribute to the long-term academic competitiveness of universities. At MSU-IIT, this function is carried by the Academic Personnel Development Program, which governs faculty scholarship application, review, approval, and related obligations under institutional policy (MSU-IIT, 2021). Solving problems in this workflow therefore matters not only for administrative convenience, but also for faculty progression, department planning, and institutional capability.

However, the scholarship workflow handled through the IASP remains dependent on manual, paper-based processes. Faculty applicants are required to prepare physical document sets, secure endorsements from multiple offices, and submit requirements through in-person routing. After submission, no centralized digital mechanism exists to verify completeness, show the current status of an application, track stage transitions, or provide a unified record of panel action. These conditions make the process vulnerable to delays, repetitive follow-up, case ambiguity, and record fragmentation.

Digital systems have already been adopted in higher education to centralize records, structure multistep workflows, and improve administrative service delivery (World Bank, 2022). Institutions also use spreadsheets, email, shared folders, and other digital tools to reduce the burden of manual file handling. These tools show that the need for digital support is already recognized. Yet they do not directly solve the needs of the IASP workflow at MSU-IIT. They support isolated tasks, but they do not enforce sequence, do not restrict actions by institutional role, and do not maintain a single authoritative view of an application's status across its life cycle.

The specific limitation of these existing tools is that they cannot enforce the role-bounded and policy-driven workflow required by APDP scholarship administration. A case may be forwarded before a prior endorsement is fully completed. A document may exist in one office folder while deliberation notes exist in another record space. Applicants and evaluators may not share a common view of the same case at the same time. These weaknesses are the structural consequence of using disconnected tools for a process that requires integrated workflow logic.

This study addresses that gap by proposing the development of the Faculty Scholarship Grant Management and Evaluation System (FSMES), a web-based platform that digitizes and structures the scholarship application submission and panel evaluation process within the IASP workflow at MSU-IIT. Through FSMES, faculty applicants can submit their applications and supporting documents digitally, while the Academic Scholarship Panel can use a centralized interface to organize submissions, review records, record decisions, and monitor case progress. The system incorporates role-based permissions, centralized records management, and workflow-sensitive status handling so that the documented weaknesses of the current IASP process can be addressed through a purpose-built digital intervention.

Research Questions

This study seeks to answer the following questions:

1. What workflow inefficiencies, pain points, and documentary bottlenecks exist in the IASP scholarship application and evaluation process at MSU-IIT?
2. What process model and priority user needs can be derived from the documented workflow problems?
3. What system architecture, user roles, and functional requirements are necessary for the development of FSMES?
4. How can a web-based FSMES prototype be developed to support digital application submission, centralized document management, and role-bounded evaluation?
5. To what extent does the FSMES prototype meet its intended functional requirements and usability expectations during evaluation?

1.3 Objective of the Study
General Objective

The general objective of this study is to develop the Faculty Scholarship Grant Management and Evaluation System (FSMES), a web-based platform that digitizes and structures the scholarship application submission and panel evaluation workflow between faculty applicants and the Academic Scholarship Panel within the IASP process at Mindanao State University-Iligan Institute of Technology.

Specific Objectives

1. To identify the workflow inefficiencies, pain points, and documentary bottlenecks in the IASP scholarship application and evaluation process at MSU-IIT.
2. To develop a proposed process model and a set of priority user needs for the IASP scholarship workflow.
3. To design the system architecture, user role structure, and functional requirements of FSMES in alignment with the documented APDP workflow.
4. To develop a functional web-based FSMES prototype that incorporates digital application submission, centralized records handling, and workflow-based status support.
5. To evaluate the functional correctness and usability of the FSMES prototype using functionality testing and user-based evaluation.

1.4 Significance of the Study

This study is significant because it addresses an actual administrative problem within faculty scholarship governance at MSU-IIT rather than a purely theoretical issue. The value of FSMES lies in its capacity to reduce procedural ambiguity, centralize records, and impose workflow discipline on a process that currently depends on manual coordination. The following stakeholders may benefit from the study.

Faculty Applicants. Faculty applicants benefit directly from a platform that reduces the physical burden of application submission and improves status visibility. Instead of relying on manual delivery and informal follow-up, applicants are able to submit requirements digitally and monitor the progress of their application at critical stages of the workflow. This reduces uncertainty and supports the broader goal of more responsive and user-oriented administrative services in higher education (World Bank, 2022).

Academic Scholarship Panel. Members of the Academic Scholarship Panel benefit from a centralized environment for organizing applications, reviewing documentary completeness, recording evaluation outcomes, and managing pending cases. The system reduces dependence on separate folders, scattered communications, and manual status checking. As a result, the evaluation process becomes more orderly, traceable, and administratively defensible in relation to the panel's formal screening and evaluation responsibilities under APDP policy (MSU-IIT, 2021).

IASP Administrators and the Office of the Vice Chancellor for Academic Affairs. Although these offices are not the two primary user groups implemented in FSMES, they may benefit indirectly from a consolidated repository of application records, supporting documents, and decision histories generated through the system. This reduces the time spent reconciling information across paper files and disconnected digital tools. It also improves the retrievability of records for monitoring, compliance checking, and institutional reporting related to APDP implementation under the documented institutional process (MSU-IIT, 2021).

Mindanao State University-Iligan Institute of Technology. At the institutional level, FSMES offers a practical model for how a policy-bound faculty development workflow can be translated into a structured digital system. The study contributes to administrative modernization by showing that a complex, role-sensitive process can be supported by a purpose-built platform rather than by fragmented manual practices, which is consistent with broader higher education digital transformation goals (World Bank, 2022).

Information Technology Developers. For student developers and practitioners working on higher education information systems, FSMES provides a concrete case of applied system development in a real institutional setting. The project demonstrates how workflow enforcement, role-based access control, status monitoring, and user-centered design can be combined in a solution that responds to a specific administrative problem rather than a generic software brief, which aligns with the problem-to-prototype logic of Design Sprint (Knapp et al., 2016).

Future Researchers. Future researchers may use this study as a basis for extending the system to later administrative tiers of the APDP, strengthening mobile access, integrating analytics, or adapting the approach to other faculty development workflows in similar state university contexts. The study also provides a practical example of how Design Sprint can be used in undergraduate applied capstone development when the goal is a defensible institutional solution rather than theory validation (Knapp et al., 2016).

1.5 Scope of the Study

This study focuses on the design, development, and evaluation of the Faculty Scholarship Grant Management and Evaluation System (FSMES), a web-based platform for the application submission and panel evaluation stages of the Academic Personnel Development Program at MSU-IIT. Specifically, the study concentrates on the portion of the workflow in which faculty applicants formally submit scholarship applications and supporting documents, and the Academic Scholarship Panel receives, reviews, and records action on those submissions.

The system accommodates two primary user groups: faculty applicants and Academic Scholarship Panel members. For faculty applicants, the system provides an interface for application submission, document uploading, and status monitoring. For panel users, the system provides an interface for application organization, review handling, decision recording, and workflow-sensitive status management. The study also incorporates role-based access logic so that users can access only the data and functions relevant to their responsibilities in the IASP workflow.

The study is conducted within the institutional setting of MSU-IIT during the Second Semester of Academic Year 2025-2026. System development and evaluation are limited to the capstone period and to the actual stakeholders involved in the selected workflow scope. Evaluation involves six participants composed of five faculty applicants and one Academic Scholarship Panel member.

1.6 Limitations of the Study

This study is subject to several limitations. First, FSMES is limited to the application submission and panel evaluation stage of the APDP workflow. It does not automate the full institutional chain beyond the IASP stage, including endorsement routing through the Department Chairperson and College Dean, the recommendation process of the Academic Planning Committee, the issuance of the Chancellor's Special Order, and the final confirmation process at the Board of Regents. It also does not cover the post-approval compliance documents required by the official guidelines, such as the health certificate, certificate of mental and emotional stability, institute clearance, notarized fellowship agreement, surety bond, and proof of APDP orientation.

Second, FSMES is designed specifically for the policy context and workflow requirements of the APDP at MSU-IIT. As such, the system is not intended for direct deployment in other universities or scholarship programs without process-level and policy-level modification. Its rules, user roles, and workflow structure are tailored to the institutional realities defined for this capstone.

Third, the evaluation involves only six participants. Although this size is acceptable for prototype-level assessment within a capstone study, any findings reflect the perceptions of a limited stakeholder group and should not be interpreted as full-scale implementation evidence.

Finally, the intended output of the study is a functional prototype rather than a full production deployment. While the prototype is intended to demonstrate the core workflow and user functions of the proposed system, full institutional use would still require further development, deployment planning, security review, and integration with relevant university processes.

1.7 Conceptual Framework

This study is guided by a five-phase conceptual framework based on the Design Sprint methodology of Understand, Define, Sketch, Prototype, and Test (Knapp et al., 2016). The framework is appropriate because the problem addressed by FSMES is not merely technical. It is an applied workflow problem that requires every major design decision to be grounded in the documented experiences of actual users within the IASP process.

The Understand phase engages the primary stakeholders of the study, namely faculty applicants and Academic Scholarship Panel members, to document the existing workflow, identify pain points, and clarify where delays, ambiguity, and record fragmentation occur. Human-centred design literature emphasizes that direct user involvement is necessary to ground system requirements in the actual context of use rather than in designer assumptions (Maguire, 2001). This phase produces the initial evidence base of the study.

The Define phase transforms the documented pain points into a bounded design problem. Human-centred design methods support the translation of user and workflow evidence into explicit requirements and design directions rather than vague problem descriptions (Maguire, 2001). The purpose of this phase is to prevent the project from becoming a generic scholarship portal and to ensure that FSMES remains tied to the specific workflow failures of the IASP process.

The Sketch phase converts the confirmed user needs into low-fidelity interface ideas, feature groupings, and process flows. Low-fidelity prototyping is valuable at this stage because it allows teams to explore and refine interface options before implementation commits them to the wrong features or interaction patterns (Snyder, 2003). The core discipline of this phase is that no proposed feature is included unless it directly addresses a documented user problem from the earlier phases.

The Prototype phase translates the selected solution concept into a functional web-based prototype. Usability engineering supports the use of working or testable representations so that interface problems can be observed and corrected before full deployment (Nielsen, 1994). The prototype embodies the major system decisions of the study, including digital application submission, centralized case organization, role-based access control, and workflow-sensitive status support.

The Test phase evaluates the prototype using functionality testing and user-based usability assessment. Standardized post-study usability measures such as the PSSUQ provide structured evidence of user perceptions after task completion, while small-sample usability testing can still reveal major interface issues when the participants are carefully selected (Lewis, 2002; Nielsen, 1994). The purpose of this final phase is to determine whether the prototype adequately addresses the documented workflow problems that justify the project. The outputs of this phase include functionality test results, PSSUQ scores, and a validated FSMES prototype.

1.8 Definition of Terms

Academic Personnel Development Program (APDP). In this study, APDP refers to the institutional policy framework of MSU-IIT that governs faculty scholarship and fellowship grants, including eligibility, endorsement, evaluation, and related obligations. The term is used operationally as the source of the procedural rules that FSMES needs to reflect.

Academic Scholarship Panel / Institute Academic Scholarship Panel (IASP). In this study, IASP refers to the institutional body responsible for receiving, reviewing, interviewing, and evaluating faculty scholarship applications under the APDP. The term denotes the principal evaluating authority represented in the system.

Audit Trail. In this study, audit trail refers to the traceable record of workflow actions such as submission, review, status change, and decision recording that supports case monitoring and accountability.

Design Sprint. Design Sprint refers to the five-phase user-centered development approach consisting of Understand, Define, Sketch, Prototype, and Test (Knapp et al., 2016). In this study, it serves as the methodology used to move from documented workflow problems to a system prototype.

Faculty Applicant. Faculty applicant refers to a faculty member of MSU-IIT who applies for a scholarship or fellowship grant under the APDP and who forms one of the two primary user groups of the system. In FSMES, this role is operationally limited to application submission, document upload, and application status viewing within the covered workflow scope.

Faculty Scholarship Grant Management and Evaluation System (FSMES). FSMES refers to the web-based system proposed in this study to digitize and structure the application submission and panel evaluation workflow of faculty scholarship grants within the IASP process at MSU-IIT. The term covers the proposed prototype as an integrated platform for submission, review, role-based access, and centralized status handling.

Functional Prototype. Functional prototype refers to the working version of FSMES intended for the study period. It is capable of demonstrating the core features of the proposed system but is not considered a full production deployment for university-wide operational use.

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC). RBAC refers to the access control approach in which user permissions are assigned according to institutional role rather than by unrestricted individual access (Sandhu et al., 1996). In FSMES, RBAC is implemented so that faculty applicants and panel users can access only the functions and information relevant to their workflow responsibilities.

Workflow Enforcement. Workflow enforcement refers to the system's ability to structure actions according to the approved process sequence and role boundaries of the IASP scholarship procedure. In this study, the term distinguishes FSMES from ad hoc tools that can store information but cannot enforce stage-based administrative logic.
